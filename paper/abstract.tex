\begin{abstract}
Encrypted traffic classification identifies applications and services without inspecting packet payloads, supporting network security, service management, and traffic measurement. Modern deep representations perform strongly on static, closed-set traffic benchmarks, yet class-incremental encrypted traffic classification remains challenging when new applications continually emerge and historical data cannot be fully retained. Flows from the same application can vary substantially, while flows from different applications can appear similar because they share protocols, cloud services, or third-party components. Consequently, the relevance of historical knowledge varies across flows, whereas existing replay and distillation methods typically use fixed global weights that cannot adapt at the flow level. We find that the reconstruction error of an autoencoder trained on historical traffic provides a per-flow signal of compatibility with historical class manifolds. Based on this finding, we propose Reconstruction-Aware Routing (RAR). For each flow, RAR converts its reconstruction error into a continuous old/new compatibility score and uses the same score to control both historical-knowledge preservation and new-knowledge acquisition during training, as well as the soft integration of a frozen historical-class path and a trainable new-class path during inference. Further logit calibration enables joint prediction over a unified traffic-class space. Experiments on six public datasets show that RAR reduces forgetting by 35.5\%--67.5\% relative to the strongest evaluated classical CIL baseline and improves mixed-class accuracy by up to 11.7 percentage points. Multi-step and replay-budget experiments further support reconstruction-conditioned old/new balancing.
\end{abstract}
