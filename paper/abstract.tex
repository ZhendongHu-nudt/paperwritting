\begin{abstract}
Encrypted-traffic classifiers must continuously absorb emerging applications and threats without retraining from scratch. Although class-incremental learning (CIL) enables continual model updates, existing methods typically rely on replay, knowledge distillation, or regularization to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. However, these mechanisms remain largely sample-agnostic, lacking a way to estimate whether an incoming sample is more closely associated with previously acquired or newly emerging knowledge.

We observe that an autoencoder trained on frozen encoder features of replayed old-class samples reconstructs old samples with lower error than new ones. This observation suggests that reconstruction error provides an input-dependent signal of whether a sample is better represented by previously acquired or newly arriving knowledge. We call this signal \emph{knowledge ownership}.

Based on this observation, we propose Reconstruction-Aware Routing (RAR), which converts reconstruction error into a sample-level routing score and uses it to softly weight two dedicated classification heads. A lightweight logit calibration module further aligns the dual-head outputs into a unified prediction space.

Experiments on six public encrypted-traffic datasets demonstrate that RAR reduces forgetting by 35.5\%--67.5\% over competitive CIL baselines while improving mixed-class accuracy by up to 11.7 percentage points. Ablation studies further show the respective contributions of knowledge ownership estimation, dual-head soft routing, and logit calibration. These results support reconstruction-derived routing as a useful sample-aware mechanism for encrypted-traffic class-incremental learning.


\end{abstract}
